The RX-8 vs the RX-7

The Mazda RX-8 is a significant depature from all of the generations of the RX-7, but most distinctly from the last generation of RX-7, the FD. To maximize power and torque, the 13B featured in the FD RX-7 had not one but two sequential turbochargers. While these had their own problems, (the turbos had a habit of having a very sudden switch, resulting in an unexpected power increase that could lead to unexpected loss of traction) the turbos defined the RX-7 line as a whole towards the end of its production.

The FD RX-7 was iconic, between its performance and its looks, and as such the RX-8 had a lot to live up to as its successor. According to many, the RX-8 failed in this goal. To improve emissions, out went the turbos and in came a revised version of the 13B. This revision of the 13B, known as the Renesis, moved the exhaust ports from being on the rotor housing (so the tip of the rotor swept across them) to the side housings. This was done to improve emissions and fuel economy, which sounds like a good thing!

Unfortunately, this came with several downsides. Exhaust scavenging suffered, as the exhaust route was more complicated compared to having the exhaust ports directly on the rotor housing. This in turn limited the ultimate power potential of the engine, meaning the RX-7 would not only be more powerful from the factory but had more tuning potential to unlock even more performance. And while the improved fuel economy was there, it wasn't significant enough to turn the RX-8 into a economy car. In some cases, the fuel economy between a FD RX-7 and a RX-8 is within the margin of error.

It's not all doom and gloom for the Renesis however. From the factory the 6-port version has redline at 9K and the engine has no problem reaching it. The Renesis is also effectively fully tuned from the factory, which means you don't have to spend time and money on tuning it yourself (although of course this is a downside if you look forward to doing that process yourself).

The RX-8, with its many flaws, is significantly cheaper than just about any RX-7, regardless of generation, while being the most modern car to be powered only by a rotary. This means it a very good entry into owning a rotary car, despite the fact an RX-8 cannot treated the same as a RX-7. Regardless of the iteration, driving a rotary engine is an experience matched by no other and (in my opinion) is worth putting up with the downsides of the engine.
